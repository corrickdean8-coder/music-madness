Lesson 3 — Artist Spotlight: Chris Thile Advanced Mandolin · Signature Techniques of a Genre-Defying Virtuoso
⌄

Chris Thile has redefined what the mandolin can do — blending bluegrass, chamber music, jazz, and indie folk into a singular voice that no one has replicated. Studying his approach unlocks chromatic melody lines, displaced chop rhythms, and cross-string tremolo techniques that most mandolinists never explore. This spotlight gives you his biography, three essential recordings, and three defining techniques to work into your playing immediately.

Born: 1981, Oceanside, California
Key Groups: Nickel Creek, Punch Brothers
Radio Host: "Live From Here" (successor to Prairie Home Companion)

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SIGNATURE TECHNIQUE #1: CHROMATIC PASSING TONES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Thile routinely uses half-step approaches to target notes,
giving his lines a jazz-influenced, forward-moving quality.

Instead of jumping G → B directly, he walks chromatically:
G → G# → A → A# → B

Tab (ascending chromatic approach in G major, E string):
e|--0--1--2--3--5--7--8--10--|
a|---------------------------|

Descending (landing on open A):
e|--10--8--7--5--4--3--2--0--|
a|---------------------------|

Strict alternate picking. Every note must ring clean.
Target BPM: Start at 60, push to 140 over two weeks.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SIGNATURE TECHNIQUE #2: THE DISPLACED CHOP
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Standard bluegrass chop lands squarely on beats 2 and 4.
Thile displaces the chop rhythmically — landing on the "and"
of 2 or syncopating across the barline, creating tension.

4/4 rhythm pattern (G chord, displaced chop marked with X):

Beat: 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +
↓ X ↓ X
strum CHOP strum CHOP
(early — on "and of 3")

Practice with metronome at 70 BPM until the displacement
feels natural, then push to 110 BPM.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SIGNATURE TECHNIQUE #3: CROSS-STRING TREMOLO
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Thile executes tremolo not on a single string but rolls
the pick rapidly across adjacent string pairs, producing
a harp-like shimmer unique to his style.

Tab (D chord, cross-string tremolo on strings 1 & 2):
e|--2~2~2~2~2~2--|
B|--3~3~3~3~3~3--|

Alternate the pick between e and B strings in rapid succession.
The strings should blur into a sustained chord shimmer.
Target: clean 16th notes at 100 BPM before increasing speed.

Technique: Begin with Technique #1 — the chromatic scale. Use a metronome and strict alternate picking (down-up-down-up). Clarity before speed: every note must speak cleanly at 60 BPM before advancing. Next, isolate Technique #2: set your metronome to 70 BPM, play a G chord, and practice the displaced chop grid until it stops feeling "wrong." Finally, drill the cross-string tremolo on a D chord with a drone note underneath on the open A string for tonal grounding.

🎵 Practice: Days 1–2: Chromatic G scale (ascending + descending), 60 BPM, 10 min/day. Days 3–4: Displaced chop grid, 70–90 BPM, 10 min/day. Days 5–7: Listen to "Passades" with headphones — identify each of the three techniques in the recording. At the advanced level, ear training is not optional.

Instrument for this lesson: mandolin

Lesson 5 — Walking the Blues: 12-Bar Progression and Bass Movement

Intermediate Bass · Theory Challenge
∨

The 12-bar blues is the engine of American roots music — alive in blues, rock, country, and bluegrass shuffle alike. As a bassist, your job goes beyond holding roots: you walk the listener through each chord change with intention and groove. Understanding this progression at a theory level will unlock hundreds of songs and sessions.

Standard Bass Tuning: E A D G  |  Tempo: ♩= 75 BPM

G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|

E |--3--5--3--2--------------------------|
   G  B  G  F#     D  F#  (walk to D)

Technique: A walking bass line connects chord roots using scale tones and chromatic neighbour notes. Each bar starts on the root of the active chord — G (3rd fret, E string), C (3rd fret, A string), D (5th fret, A string). The repeating shape 3–5–3–2 spells out root–5th–root–flatted 7th, giving each chord its bluesy dominant flavour. At bar 5, simply shift to the A string — same fingering, new string, new chord. The turnaround (bars 11–12) plays one bar of G7 then walks up to D (frets 5–7 on A string), cueing the band to loop back to the top.

Theory Challenge — 5 Questions:

  1. In a 12-bar blues in G, name the three chords and their Roman numerals (I, IV, V).
  2. What note is added to a major chord to make it a dominant 7th — and why does that note create tension?
  3. The bass line plays G–B–G–F# over G7. Which chord tones are those (root, 3rd, 5th, or b7)?
  4. Transpose the whole progression to A. What three chords would you play?
  5. What is the name for the last two bars of a 12-bar blues that signal the start of a new chorus?

Practice: Loop bars 1-4 at 60 BPM for 5 minutes. Add bars 5-6 (C7), then the full 12-bar form. Goal: no hesitation at chord changes. Push to 80 BPM once the walk feels locked in.

Lesson 6 The Delta Drone Lick Advanced Open-String Drone Blues Technique v

Delta blues players like Robert Johnson and Son House built their sound around hypnotic open-string drones - one note ringing steadily while melody crawls above or below it. Most ukulele players never venture into this territory, but the technique is devastatingly effective: your open A string (string 1) becomes a pedal tone while a chromatic blues descent unfolds on string 2. This is one of those licks that makes listeners stop and say "wait - that's a ukulele?"

Lick of the Day: "The Delta Drone" (Key of A Minor)

Ukulele - GCEA Tuning - Slow Blues Feel | = 65 BPM

     [1] [2] [3] [4] [5] [6] [7] [8]
A |--0---0---0---0---0---0---0---0--| (DRONE: always open)
E |--5---5---4---4---3---3---2---0--| (melody descends)
C |----------------------------------|
G |----------------------------------|
Fret to Pitch on E string:
  • Fret 5 = A (unison with drone)
  • Fret 4 = G# (sharpened 7th - tension)
  • Fret 3 = G (flat 7th - the blue note)
  • Fret 2 = F# (major 6th - color)
  • Open = E (perfect 5th - resolution)

Play as pairs: pinch A+E[5], then A+E[5], then A+E[4], etc. The chromatic slide A-G#-G-F#-E is the blues in motion.

Technique:
  1. Right hand - Pinch your thumb (A string) and index finger (E string) simultaneously on each pair. Keep the thumb anchored; never let the drone break.
  2. Left hand - Fret the E string at position 5. Each pair of beats, slide DOWN one fret: 5 to 4 to 3 to 2 to open. Do not rush - let the dissonance breathe.
  3. The chromatic tension - G# (fret 4) is the sharpened 7th against the A drone. G natural (fret 3) is the blue note - the flat 7. F# (fret 2) adds a major 6th color. Open E is your resolution, the perfect 5th. Each step tells a story.
  4. Timing - Think of it as two eighth notes per fret position. Slow and deliberate. The Delta blues never rushed.
  5. Extension - After the open E, add a hammer-on back to fret 2 (F#) then resolve to open A for a complete phrase turnaround.
Practice:

Run the lick at 55 BPM for 5 minutes straight - focus entirely on keeping the A drone unbroken and the left-hand slides even. Then add the hammer-on extension. Finally, record yourself and listen back: the drone should feel like a heartbeat. Target: clean execution at 80 BPM within one week.

Instrument for this lesson: ukulele

Lesson 7 — The Birth of Slide Guitar

Beginner Delta Blues Origins & the Bottleneck Revolution
∨

Before rock and roll, before electric guitar, there was the sound of a glass bottle neck sliding across steel strings on a Mississippi front porch — and it changed the world. Slide guitar, born from the Delta Blues tradition of the early 1900s, is one of the most emotionally raw and distinctly American sounds ever created. Understanding where it came from is the first step to making that voice your own.

Open G Tuning (Low → High): D - G - D - G - B - D

Standard E: E - A - D - G - B - E
Open G:     D - G - D - G - B - D
            ↓   =   ↓   =   =   ↓
(tune down 2 steps, same, down 2 steps, same, same, down 2 steps)

Basic Slide Position — 12th Fret (open G chord):

e |----12----|
B |----12----|
G |----12----|
D |----12----|
G |----12----|
D |----12----|

Lay the slide lightly across all 6 strings directly above the 12th fret.
Do NOT press down — just rest it on the strings and strum.

Slide to 14th fret = A major | Slide to 17th fret = C major

The Story:

Slide guitar traces its roots to West African musical traditions carried to America — where single-string instruments called diddley bows were played by pressing a bone or smooth stone against the string to create sustained, singing tones. By the early 1900s, Black musicians in the Mississippi Delta were breaking bottle necks, repurposing knife blades, and fashioning metal rings to produce a sound unlike anything heard before: a guitar that could cry, moan, and wail like a human voice.

Charley Patton — the "Father of the Delta Blues" — was the first great slide guitarist on record, stomping and slapping his guitar like a percussionist while his slide wept through the strings. Son House played with ferocious, almost violent intensity, dragging a metal ring across the strings as he preached the blues. Then came Robert Johnson — who recorded only 29 songs between 1936–37 — and those recordings became some of the most influential music in history. His "Dust My Broom," "Crossroads Blues," and "Come On in My Kitchen" carried the slide guitar sound to every corner of the world.

The British Invasion brought it full circle: Muddy Waters electrified Delta slide in Chicago; Keith Richards heard it and rewired the Rolling Stones; Jimmy Page felt it and built "In My Time of Dying." Slide guitar didn't just shape the blues — it shaped all of modern music.

🎵 Practice:

Tune your guitar to Open G (D-G-D-G-B-D). Place a glass or metal slide on your ring finger (or pinky). Rest it lightly across all 6 strings at the 12th fret — no pressure, just touch. Strum slowly and listen to that open G chord sustain. Now slide up two frets to the 14th fret (A chord), pause, then slide back to the 12th. Focus entirely on placement: the slide must sit directly above the fret wire for clean intonation. Practice this 12→14→12 movement 10 times at a meditative pace. This single gesture — that simple slide — is the DNA of 100 years of American music.

Instrument for this lesson: slide-guitar
