Lesson 1 — G Major Chord
Beginner The cornerstone chord
⌄

The G major chord is the cornerstone of folk and bluegrass guitar. Almost every song in these genres uses G, and it's the perfect starting point. Master this chord and you'll be playing along to hundreds of songs within days.

e |---3---| B |---3---| G |---0---| D |---0---| A |---2---| E |---3---|

Fingering: Middle finger on low E, 3rd fret. Ring finger on A string, 2nd fret. Pinky on B string, 3rd fret. Index on high e, 3rd fret (or leave open for easier shape).

🎵 Practice: Strum slowly 4 times, counting "1-2-3-4." Aim for every string to ring clearly. If you hear buzzing, press fingertips right behind the fret wire.
Lesson 2 — D Major Chord
Beginner The perfect partner to G
⌄

D major pairs perfectly with G for folk and country songs. Together, these two chords are the backbone of bluegrass rhythm. Once you can switch smoothly between G and D, you're ready to play real songs.

e |---2---| B |---3---| G |---2---| D |---0---| A |---x---| E |---x---|

Fingering: Only strum strings 4 through 1. Index finger on G string 2nd fret, middle on high e 2nd fret, ring on B string 3rd fret. The x means don't play that string.

🎵 Practice: Switch between G and D every 4 beats. This is the backbone of hundreds of folk songs — try playing along to any simple folk tune using just these two chords.
Lesson 3 — Travis Picking Pattern
Intermediate Signature fingerpicking
⌄

Travis picking is the signature fingerpicking style of country and folk guitar. The technique creates a beautiful interplay between a steady bass line and melodic treble strings — giving you the feel of two instruments at once.

e |---3-------3-------3-------3---| B |---3-------3-------3-------3---| G |---0-------0-------0-------0---| D |---0-0---0---0---0-0---0---0---| A |---2-------2-------2-------2---| E |---3---3-------3---3---3-------|

Technique: Your thumb alternates between bass strings (E and A), while fingers pluck treble strings on the offbeat. The thumb is the rhythmic anchor; fingers add melody.

🎵 Practice: Start at 60 BPM with a metronome. Thumb on beats 1 and 3, fingers on 2 and 4. Increase speed only when the pattern feels completely natural. Aim for 100 BPM.

Lesson 1 — G Tuning & Basic Roll
Beginner Starting from scratch
⌄

The 5-string banjo in open G tuning: g-D-G-B-D (5th string is high g). All strings open = G major chord. The banjo's bright, ringing tone is the hallmark of bluegrass music.

Strings: g D G B D 5 4 3 2 1 Roll: |---0-------0---| |-------0-------| |---0-------0---| |-------0-------| |0-----------0--|

Technique: Thumb (T) plays strings 5,4,3. Index (I) plays string 2. Middle (M) plays string 1. Scruggs roll: T-I-T-M-T-I-T-M. Practice slowly — this is the foundation of everything in bluegrass.

🎵 Practice: With all strings open, play the roll 8 times without stopping. Focus on even rhythm, not speed. Speed comes naturally with repetition.
Lesson 2 — The Forward Roll
Beginner Most common bluegrass roll
⌄

The forward roll is the most common roll pattern in bluegrass banjo. It moves forward across the strings in a flowing pattern that drives the rhythm and creates the characteristic bluegrass sound Earl Scruggs made famous.

Pattern: T-I-M repeating (8 notes per measure) T = Thumb (strings 5, 4, or 3) I = Index (string 2) M = Middle (string 1) g |---0---------------0-----------| D |-------0---------------0-------| G |-----------0---------------0---| B |---0---0-------0---0-------0---| D | 0 0 |

Pattern: Each 8-note roll covers two beats of 4/4 time. Keep the motion smooth, not jerky. Your wrist should float — never tense your hand.

🎵 Practice: Play 4 forward rolls in a row without stopping. Start at 80 BPM. Increase by 5 BPM each day until you reach 120 BPM.
Lesson 3 — Clawhammer Basic Stroke
Intermediate Old-time folk style
⌄

Clawhammer is the old-time folk style of banjo — predating Scruggs by generations. It uses a downward stroke followed by a thumb brush, creating a driving "bum-ditty" pattern that feels like a conversation between melody and rhythm.

D |---0-------0-------0---| Ditty (5th string thumb) G |---0-------0-------0---| B |---0---0---0---0---0---| D |---0-----------0-------| g |-------0-----------0---| Bum (downstroke brush)

Technique: Curl your hand like a claw. Strike DOWN with the back of your index/middle fingernail, then immediately brush the 5th string with your thumb. One fluid motion — down, then thumb.

🎵 Practice: Say "bum-ditty bum-ditty" out loud as you play. BUM = the downstroke, DITTY = the thumb. Start very slowly — this technique takes weeks to feel natural, but it's incredibly rewarding.

Lesson 1 — Tuning & G Chord
Beginner Your first mandolin chord
⌄

Mandolin is tuned GDAE — same as violin, with 8 strings in 4 pairs (courses) tuned in unison. The bright, cutting tone makes it the lead melody instrument in bluegrass. Tune with a chromatic tuner app.

e |---0---| A |---0---| D |---0---| G |---0---|

Open G Chord: All strings open! Strumming all four strings without fretting gives a full G major chord. Use a medium-weight pick for a versatile sound.

🎵 Practice: Strum 8 times in a row with consistent pressure. Listen for buzzing — if you hear it, the two strings of a course aren't being pressed evenly.
Lesson 2 — D Chord & The Chop
Beginner The signature rhythm technique
⌄

The chop chord is the signature rhythm technique in bluegrass mandolin. That sharp, percussive "chk" sound on beats 2 and 4 drives the rhythm in a bluegrass band. It's the sound Bill Monroe made famous.

e |---2---| A |---3---| D |---4---| G |---4---|

Chop Technique: Fret the chord firmly, strum DOWN sharply, then immediately release pressure (keep fingers touching strings to mute). This creates the sharp "chk" sound. Muting is as important as the strum.

🎵 Practice: Chop on beats 2 and 4. Count: "1—CHOP— 3—CHOP." Play open G on 1 and 3, chop on 2 and 4 — that's full bluegrass rhythm!
Lesson 3 — G Major Scale
Intermediate The key to all melodies
⌄

The G major scale is essential for playing melodies in bluegrass and folk music. Since most sessions are in G, knowing this scale unlocks the ability to pick out melodies by ear and improvise lead lines.

e |---7---5------| A |---7---5---4--| D |---7---5---4--| G |---5---4---2--| Notes: G - A - B - C - D - E - F# - G

Technique: Play from the G string upward. Use alternate picking: down, up, down, up. Keep pick movements small and controlled — economy of motion is the key to speed.

🎵 Practice: Play the scale up and back down, 10 times at 80 BPM. Increase to 90 BPM after a few days. After a week, try picking out "Cripple Creek" — it's all in this scale!

Music Theory

Understanding the language beneath the music — scales, progressions, and the building blocks of folk, blues & bluegrass.

1. The Blues Scale
The sound that defines American music
⌄

The blues scale is the foundation of blues, rock, and much of folk. It's a minor pentatonic scale with an added "blue note" — a flattened 5th that gives the blues its bittersweet sound. In G: G, B♭, C, D♭, D, F.

G Blues Scale on Guitar: e |---3---6---8---9---10---11---| B |---3---6---8---9---10-------| G |---3---5---6---7---8--------| D |---3---5---6---7---8--------| A |---3---5---6---7------------| E |---3---6---8----------------| Notes: G Bb C Db D F G

The "blue note" (D♭/C#) sits between C and D. Bending strings up to or through this note is the hallmark of blues guitar. Try playing this scale and experimenting with string bends — that's where the emotion lives.

2. The 12-Bar Blues
The universal language of blues musicians
⌄

The 12-bar blues is the most important chord progression in American music. Once you know it, you can jam with any blues musician in any key. In G, the three chords are G (I), C (IV), and D (V).

Bar: | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | Chord:| G | G | G | G | Bar: | 5 | 6 | 7 | 8 | Chord:| C | C | G | G | Bar: | 9 | 10 | 11 | 12 | Chord:| D | C | G | D | (Bar 12 = "turnaround" -- leads back to bar 1)

Each bar is typically 4 beats. The "quick change" variation goes to C in bar 2. The turnaround in bars 11-12 creates tension that resolves back to the beginning — this circular movement is what makes the 12-bar blues so addictive.

3. The Pentatonic Scale
Five notes, infinite possibilities
⌄

The pentatonic (five-note) scale is used in virtually every genre worldwide. The minor pentatonic (G, B♭, C, D, F) gives a blues/rock feel. The major pentatonic (G, A, B, D, E) gives a brighter country/folk sound.

G Minor Pentatonic (open position): e |---3---6---| B |---3---6---| G |---3---5---| D |---3---5---| A |---3---5---| E |---3---6---| G Major Pentatonic: e |---0---2---3---| B |---0---2---3---| G |---0---2-------| D |---0---2-------| A |---0---2-------| E |---0---2---3---|

Memorize the minor pentatonic box shape first — play it over a 12-bar blues and it sounds great every time. The major pentatonic works beautifully in country, folk, and bluegrass for warmer, melodic sounds.

4. The I–IV–V Progression
Three chords, a thousand songs
⌄

The I–IV–V progression is the foundation of folk, country, and blues. In G: I = G, IV = C, V = D. These three chords create complete harmonic tension and resolution — the backbone of nearly all folk and bluegrass songs.

Key of G: I = G IV = C V = D Key of D: I = D IV = G V = A Key of A: I = A IV = D V = E Key of C: I = C IV = F V = G Key of E: I = E IV = A V = B Simple Folk Progression in G: | G | G | C | G | | G | D | G | D | Repeat and you have hundreds of folk songs!

The V chord creates tension that "wants" to resolve back to the I chord — the fundamental principle of Western harmony. In bluegrass, the I–IV–V is played at high speed and forms the basis of songs like "Foggy Mountain Breakdown."

5. Key Signatures for Folk
The most common keys in the tradition
⌄

Folk, bluegrass, and blues favor certain keys because they work well with the open strings of guitar and banjo. These are the keys you'll encounter most at jams and sessions:

Key of G -- 1 sharp (F#) -- Most common bluegrass key Key of D -- 2 sharps (F#,C#) -- Common for fiddle tunes Key of A -- 3 sharps (F#,C#,G#) -- Great for slide guitar Key of E -- 4 sharps -- Strong blues key Key of C -- No sharps/flats -- Easy for beginners Key of Am -- Same as C major -- Folk ballads & minor songs Capo Tip: Using a capo lets you play G shapes in any key! Capo 2 = A, Capo 5 = C, Capo 7 = D

At bluegrass jams, G is king. Most songs are called in G, D, or A. The banjo and fiddle particularly favor G and D because the open strings ring beautifully in those keys. Learn G first, then D, and you can participate in most sessions.

6. Reading Tablature (Tab)
The shorthand of folk musicians
⌄

Tablature (tab) is a simplified notation system for fretted instruments. Unlike standard notation, you don't need to read music — it directly shows you where to put your fingers.

HOW TO READ GUITAR TAB: Each line = one string (top = highest, bottom = lowest) Numbers = fret number (0 = open, x = don't play) Left-right = notes in time order Stacked = play notes together (chord) e |---0---2---3------| B |---0---3---3------| G |---0---2---0------| D |---0---0---0------| A |---x---x---2------| E |---x---x---3------| Em D G Symbols: h = hammer-on p = pull-off b = bend ~ = vibrato / = slide up \ = slide down

Tab doesn't show rhythm perfectly, but shows exactly where to fret. Listen to the song and use tab as a finger-placement map. You'll quickly develop timing intuition from the music itself.

The Legends

The artists who shaped folk, blues, and bluegrass — whose music echoes through every lesson in this app.

🪕
Bill Monroe
Mandolin · Bluegrass
The "Father of Bluegrass." Monroe invented the genre by blending Appalachian old-time music with blues and jazz. His driving mandolin style and high lonesome vocal sound defined bluegrass forever. His Blue Grass Boys launched the careers of Earl Scruggs and Lester Flatt.
▶ Search on YouTube
🪕
Chris Thile
Mandolin · Newgrass / Classical
The modern mandolin virtuoso. Thile began with Nickel Creek, pushing the boundaries of acoustic music. His solo work and Punch Brothers prove the mandolin can handle Bach sonatas, jazz improvisation, and pop songwriting equally. A once-in-a-generation talent.
▶ Search on YouTube
🪘
Earl Scruggs
Banjo · Bluegrass
The man who invented 3-finger banjo style. Before Scruggs, banjo was played in the clawhammer style. His revolutionary right-hand technique — now called Scruggs style — defined the sound of bluegrass and put the banjo on the world stage. "Foggy Mountain Breakdown" is his masterpiece.
▶ Search on YouTube
🪘
Béla Fleck
Banjo · Jazz / World / Classical
Béla Fleck took the banjo where no instrument had gone before — into jazz, classical orchestras, and African rhythms. With the Flecktones, he created an entirely new genre. He has won Grammy Awards in more categories than any other musician, proving the banjo's limitless range.
▶ Search on YouTube
🎸
Doc Watson
Guitar · Folk / Flatpicking
The blind guitarist from Deep Gap, NC, who virtually invented flatpicking as a lead guitar style. Watson could play fiddle tunes at breakneck speed with dazzling precision. His warm voice and encyclopedic knowledge of traditional American music make him a national treasure.
▶ Search on YouTube
🎸
Robert Johnson
Guitar · Delta Blues
The mythic Delta blues guitarist who — legend says — sold his soul at the crossroads. Johnson recorded only 29 songs, but they became the blueprint for all rock and blues. His complex guitar style, playing bass lines and melody simultaneously, influenced everyone from Clapton to Richards.
▶ Search on YouTube
🪘
Pete Seeger
Banjo · Folk Revival
The folk icon who kept American traditional music alive through the mid-20th century. Seeger's activism and music were inseparable — "We Shall Overcome" and "Where Have All the Flowers Gone?" became civil rights anthems. He wrote the definitive guide to banjo playing.
▶ Search on YouTube
🎸
Clarence White
Guitar · Flatpicking / Country Rock
The flatpicking master who bridged traditional country and rock. White's Kentucky Colonels work set a new standard for acoustic guitar. His revolutionary B-Bender device let him mimic pedal steel bends on his Telecaster, creating a whole new style of country guitar that influenced the entire genre.
▶ Search on YouTube
