───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Thile routinely uses half-step approaches to target notes,
giving his lines a jazz-influenced, forward-moving quality.

Instead of jumping G → B directly, he walks chromatically:
G → G# → A → A# → B

Standard bluegrass chop lands squarely on beats 2 and 4.
Thile displaces the chop rhythmically — landing on the "and"

↓ X ↓ X

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Thile executes tremolo not on a single string but rolls

Lesson 5 — Walking the Blues: 12-Bar Progression and Bass Movement

Intermediate Bass · Theory Challenge
∨

The 12-bar blues is the engine of American roots music — alive in blues, rock, country, and bluegrass shuffle alike. As a bassist, your job goes beyond holding roots: you walk the listener through each chord change with intention and groove. Understanding this progression at a theory level will unlock hundreds of songs and sessions.

Walking Bass Line — 12-Bar Blues in G
Standard Bass Tuning: E A D G  |  Tempo: ♩= 75 BPM

BARS 1-4  (I7 = G7)
G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|
A |--------------------------------------|
E |--3--5--3--2--3--5--3--2-------------|
   G  B  G  F# G  B  G  F#

BARS 5-6  (IV7 = C7)
G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|
A |--3--5--3--2--3--5--3--2-------------|
E |--------------------------------------|
   C  E  C  B  C  E  C  B

BARS 7-8  (I7 = G7)  -- same as bars 1-4
G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|
A |--------------------------------------|
E |--3--5--3--2--3--5--3--2-------------|

BAR 9   (V7 = D7)
G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|
A |--5--7--5--4--------------------------|
E |--------------------------------------|
   D  F# D  C#

BAR 10  (IV7 = C7)
G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|
A |--3--5--3--2--------------------------|
E |--------------------------------------|
   C  E  C  B

BARS 11-12  (I7 to Turnaround)
G |--------------------------------------|
D |--------------------------------------|
A |------------------5--7----------------|
E |--3--5--3--2--------------------------|
   G  B  G  F#     D  F#  (walk to D)

Technique: A walking bass line connects chord roots using scale tones and chromatic neighbour notes. Each bar starts on the root of the active chord — G (3rd fret, E string), C (3rd fret, A string), D (5th fret, A string). The repeating shape 3–5–3–2 spells out root–5th–root–flatted 7th, giving each chord its bluesy dominant flavour. At bar 5, simply shift to the A string — same fingering, new string, new chord. The turnaround (bars 11–12) plays one bar of G7 then walks up to D (frets 5–7 on A string), cueing the band to loop back to the top.

Theory Challenge — 5 Questions:

  1. In a 12-bar blues in G, name the three chords and their Roman numerals (I, IV, V).
  2. What note is added to a major chord to make it a dominant 7th — and why does that note create tension?
  3. The bass line plays G–B–G–F# over G7. Which chord tones are those (root, 3rd, 5th, or b7)?
  4. Transpose the whole progression to A. What three chords would you play?
  5. What is the name for the last two bars of a 12-bar blues that signal the start of a new chorus?

Practice: Loop bars 1-4 at 60 BPM for 5 minutes. Add bars 5-6 (C7), then the full 12-bar form. Goal: no hesitation at chord changes. Push to 80 BPM once the walk feels locked in.

Lesson 6 The Delta Drone Lick Advanced Open-String Drone Blues Technique v

Delta blues players like Robert Johnson and Son House built their sound around hypnotic open-string drones - one note ringing steadily while melody crawls above or below it. Most ukulele players never venture into this territory, but the technique is devastatingly effective: your open A string (string 1) becomes a pedal tone while a chromatic blues descent unfolds on string 2. This is one of those licks that makes listeners stop and say "wait - that's a ukulele?"

Lick of the Day: "The Delta Drone" (Key of A Minor)

Ukulele - GCEA Tuning - Slow Blues Feel | = 65 BPM

     [1] [2] [3] [4] [5] [6] [7] [8]
A |--0---0---0---0---0---0---0---0--| (DRONE: always open)
E |--5---5---4---4---3---3---2---0--| (melody descends)
C |----------------------------------|
G |----------------------------------|
Fret to Pitch on E string:
  • Fret 5 = A (unison with drone)
  • Fret 4 = G# (sharpened 7th - tension)
  • Fret 3 = G (flat 7th - the blue note)
  • Fret 2 = F# (major 6th - color)
  • Open = E (perfect 5th - resolution)

Play as pairs: pinch A+E[5], then A+E[5], then A+E[4], etc. The chromatic slide A-G#-G-F#-E is the blues in motion.

Technique:
  1. Right hand - Pinch your thumb (A string) and index finger (E string) simultaneously on each pair. Keep the thumb anchored; never let the drone break.
  2. Left hand - Fret the E string at position 5. Each pair of beats, slide DOWN one fret: 5 to 4 to 3 to 2 to open. Do not rush - let the dissonance breathe.
  3. The chromatic tension - G# (fret 4) is the sharpened 7th against the A drone. G natural (fret 3) is the blue note - the flat 7. F# (fret 2) adds a major 6th color. Open E is your resolution, the perfect 5th. Each step tells a story.
  4. Timing - Think of it as two eighth notes per fret position. Slow and deliberate. The Delta blues never rushed.
  5. Extension - After the open E, add a hammer-on back to fret 2 (F#) then resolve to open A for a complete phrase turnaround.
Practice:

Run the lick at 55 BPM for 5 minutes straight - focus entirely on keeping the A drone unbroken and the left-hand slides even. Then add the hammer-on extension. Finally, record yourself and listen back: the drone should feel like a heartbeat. Target: clean execution at 80 BPM within one week.

Instrument for this lesson: ukulele

Lesson 8 — Playing in the Pocket: Rhythm Guitar for Bluegrass Jams

⌄

In a bluegrass jam, your rhythm guitar is the foundation everyone else builds on. Playing "in the pocket" means locking your strum to the beat, leaving space for soloists, and blending rather than competing. Master this skill and other musicians will always want you in their circle.

// The Boom-Chuck Pattern in G //
// Bass note on beats 1 & 3 (BOOM), chord strum on beats 2 & 4 (CHUCK)

G chord (320003): E A D G B e
                  3 2 0 0 0 3

BOOM (beat 1): Pick low E string (open G bass)
CHUCK (beat 2): Strum strings 4-1 (D-G-B-e) -- light palm mute
BOOM (beat 3): Pick A string (2nd fret = B inner voice)
CHUCK (beat 4): Strum strings 4-1

C chord (x32010):
BOOM (beat 1): Pick A string (3rd fret = C bass)
CHUCK (beat 2): Strum strings 3-1 (G-B-e)
BOOM (beat 3): Pick D string (2nd fret = E inner voice)
CHUCK (beat 4): Strum strings 3-1

D chord (xx0232):
BOOM (beat 1): Pick D string (open D bass)
CHUCK (beat 2): Strum strings 3-1 (G-B-e)
BOOM (beat 3): Pick A string (open, color tone)
CHUCK (beat 4): Strum strings 3-1

Transitional bass run G to C:
E |---|---|---|---|
A |---|---|---|-C-| (arrive on C)
D |---|---|---|---|
G |-G-|-A-|-B-|---| (walk up on beat 4 of G measure)

Technique

  1. Bass-chord separation — Keep your pick attack firm on the bass note and feather-light on the chuck. The bass note anchors the harmony; the chuck is a percussive whisper.
  2. Palm muting the chuck — Rest your picking-hand palm edge lightly on the strings near the bridge during the chord strum. This tightens the sound and prevents ring-out that muddles the lead player.
  3. Don't step on the soloist — When someone takes a break, drop your volume by 20-30%. Your job is to support, not compete.
  4. Lock to the mandolin chop — In bluegrass, the mandolin chops on beats 2 and 4. Synchronize your chuck exactly to their chop and you'll sound like one instrument.
  5. Use transitional bass runs — A quick walking run between chords (e.g., G-A-B-C on the low strings moving from G to C) signals chord changes and adds forward drive without cluttering the texture.

Practice: Set a metronome to 80 BPM. Play a I-IV-I-V-I progression (G-C-G-D-G) using the boom-chuck pattern for 10 minutes straight. Every chuck lands exactly on beats 2 and 4 — no drift. Record yourself and listen back. When it feels locked and effortless, bump to 100 BPM and repeat.

Lesson 9 — Melodic Style Banjo: The Keith Technique
⏄

Melodic style — pioneered by Bill Keith in the early 1960s — cracked open a new dimension of banjo playing by letting players run full diatonic scales note-for-note at speed, without ever repeating a string consecutively. Where Scruggs rolls arpeggiate chord shapes, melodic style weaves melody through all five strings, producing a smooth, legato-sounding single-note line. It's the technique behind fiddle-tune transcription, Newgrass soloing, and everything Béla Fleck built his career on.

 Ascending G Major Scale — Melodic (Keith) Style
 Standard G tuning: gDGBD | 5th=g 4th=D 3rd=G 2nd=B 1st=D
 RULE: No string played twice in a row.

Note: G  A  B  C  D  E  F# G
5(g)|----|----|----|----|----|----|----|-0--|
4(D)|----|-7--|----|----|----|----|----|---|
3(G)|--0-|----|-4--|----|----|----|----|----|
2(B)|----|----|----|--1-|----|--5-|----|----|
1(D)|----|----|----|----|-0--|----|--4-|----|
     T    T    I    I    M    I    M    T

String sequence: 3 → 4 → 3 → 2 → 1 → 2 → 1 → 5

Fret reference:
G3  = 3rd string, open
A3  = 4th string, 7th fret   (D + 7 semitones)
B3  = 3rd string, 4th fret   (G + 4 semitones)
C4  = 2nd string, 1st fret   (B + 1 semitone)
D4  = 1st string, open
E4  = 2nd string, 5th fret   (B + 5 semitones)
F#4 = 1st string, 4th fret   (D + 4 semitones)
G4  = 5th string, open (high drone)

Technique: Work through four stages: (1) String map first — tap the sequence 3→4→3→2→1→2→1→5 on your knee until it's automatic, no banjo needed. (2) Open strings only — play the sequence on open strings to build right-hand muscle memory before adding frets. (3) Add frets one at a time — land each fretted note cleanly at 50 BPM before chaining the full scale. (4) Both directions — learn descending (reverse the map: 5→1→2→1→2→3→4→3) and then loop ascending + descending continuously. The goal is eight perfectly even notes at any tempo — melodic style exposes every right-hand weakness instantly.

🎵 Practice: Set your metronome to 60 BPM. Play the G major scale ascending and descending for 10 clean repetitions before adding speed — increase 4 BPM at a time. Target 120 BPM by end of week. Then apply this technique to "Salt Creek" or "Whiskey Before Breakfast" — both are ideal first melodic-style tunes that immediately test your string-sequence thinking in a real musical context.

Lesson 10 — "Old Joe Clark"
Beginner Your First Bluegrass Tune · Mandolin
⌄

"Old Joe Clark" is one of the most beloved tunes in Appalachian music — a fast-moving, energetic piece played at barn dances and bluegrass jams for over a century. For beginner mandolin players, it's the perfect first full tune: the melody sits comfortably in the A position, uses only two chords (A and G), and teaches you how melody and rhythm work together on the mandolin. Once you can play "Old Joe Clark," you can walk into any bluegrass jam and join right in.

--- TUNE: Old Joe Clark (Key of A, Mandolin) --- --- Standard GDAE Tuning --- VERSE (A part): G |----0--------0-----2-0---0-2-0---| D |--2---2---2-----2-----------2---| A |--------------------------------| E |--------------------------------| G |----0--------0-----2-0---0----| D |--2---2---2-----2----------2--| A |----------------------------0-| E |------------------------------| G |----0--------0-----2-0---0-2-0---| D |--2---2---2-----2-----------2---| A |--------------------------------| E |--------------------------------| G |----0--------0-----2-0----------| D |--2---2---2-----2--------2-0----| A |---------------------------------2| E |--------------------------------| CHORUS (B part): G |--3-3-3-3-3-3-3-3--| D |--0-0-0-0-0-0-0-0--| A |-------------------| E |-------------------| G |--3-3-3-3-2-0----0-| D |----------0---2----| A |-------------------| E |-------------------| --- Chord Chart --- A chord: G chord (simplified): G |--2--| G |--0--| D |--2--| D |--0--| A |--0--| A |--0--| E |--0--| E |--2--| Form: AABB (play each part twice in sequence)

Technique: "Old Joe Clark" has two parts — the A part (verse) and the B part (chorus). Here's how to learn it step by step:

1. Learn the A part melody first. Play slowly, striking downward on each note. Focus on clean, buzz-free fretting — press just behind the fret, not on top of it.

2. Learn the B part. The chorus repeats a high G note on the first string, creating a drone-like effect characteristic of old-time melody playing.

3. Combine the parts. The traditional form is AABB — play each part twice before moving to the next.

4. Add rhythm. Once the melody is solid, practice chop chords alongside a recording — alternating A and G on beats 2 and 4.

Song Story: "Old Joe Clark" dates to at least the late 1800s, named after a man from Kentucky — though the stories around him vary wildly. The lyrics change by region (hundreds of verses, many comic), but the tune is timeless. Bill Monroe recorded it; Pete Seeger featured it in his folk songbooks; it's one of the first tunes taught at flatpicking and fiddle camps across America. Playing it connects you to a living, breathing tradition.

🎵 Practice: Start at 60 BPM with a metronome. Play the A part 4 times until memorized, then the B part 4 times. Combine in AABB form. Increase 5 BPM per day until you reach 100 BPM — that's the target jam tempo where the tune really comes alive. Record yourself at the end of the week and hear the improvement.
